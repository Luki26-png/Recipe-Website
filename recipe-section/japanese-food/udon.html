How to Make Udon

Udon, a comforting and versatile staple of Japanese cuisine, boasts thick and chewy wheat noodles. Unlike its thinner counterparts like soba or ramen, udon offers a unique textural experience. The noodles themselves are typically made from wheat flour, water, and salt, resulting in a springy and satisfying bite. Udon can be enjoyed in a variety of ways, but its essence shines in a steaming bowl of hot udon soup (kake udon). The thick noodles bathe in a light and flavorful dashi broth, often simply seasoned with soy sauce and mirin. Simple toppings like thinly sliced scallions and a sprinkle of tempura flakes add a touch of elegance and textural contrast. This classic presentation allows the inherent qualities of the udon and the savory broth to take center stage.

Beyond hot soups, udon's versatility knows no bounds. It can be served chilled in a refreshing summer salad, tossed with sesame sauce and an array of colorful vegetables. Stir-fried udon offers a hearty and flavorful option, perfect for a quick and satisfying meal. Udon can even be incorporated into hotpots or simmered in fragrant curries, showcasing its adaptability to various flavors and cooking styles. Whether enjoyed hot, cold, or incorporated into other dishes, udon's comforting texture and versatility make it a beloved element of Japanese cuisine.

Ingredients :

  • Bread Flour: This high-gluten flour gives udon its characteristic chewy texture. All-purpose flour can be used in a pinch, but the texture won't be as authentic.
  • Water: Used to bind the flour and create the dough.
  • Salt: Enhances the flavor of the noodles.
  • **(Optional)** Kombu (Kelp): A small piece of kombu can be added to the water while cooking to enhance the flavor of the noodles (remove before kneading).
  • Kombu (Kelp): The base of the flavorful dashi broth.
  • Katsuobushi (Bonito Flakes): These add umami depth to the dashi.
  • **(Optional)** Shiitake Mushrooms: These can add an earthy flavor to the broth.
  • Water: Used to make the dashi broth.
  • **(Optional)** Mirin (Sweet Rice Wine): A touch of mirin can add sweetness and depth to the broth.
  • **(Optional)** Soy Sauce: A small amount of soy sauce can be added for additional flavor (use high-quality soy sauce sparingly).
  • Scallions (optional): Thinly sliced scallions add a pop of color and freshness.
  • Tempura Flakes (optional): These crispy bits of leftover batter add a delightful textural contrast.
  • **(Optional)** Other Toppings: You can explore other toppings like narutomaki (fish cake), kamaboko (fish cake with red stripes), or a poached egg.

Instructions :

  • Prepare the Dough: In a large bowl, combine bread flour, salt, and a small piece of kombu (optional). If using kombu, pour water over it and let it sit for 30 minutes to infuse flavor. Remove the kombu before proceeding.
  • Gradually Add Water: Gradually pour water into the dry ingredients, while mixing with your fingertips or a chopstick. Form a shaggy dough.
  • Knead the Dough: Turn the dough onto a lightly floured surface and knead for 15-20 minutes, or until smooth and elastic. The dough will be quite stiff at first, but will become smoother with kneading. Add a little more water if the dough feels too dry, or a little more flour if it feels too sticky.
  • Rest the Dough: Cover the dough with plastic wrap and let it rest at room temperature for 1 hour.
  • Stretch and Fold (Optional): For a chewier texture (optional), you can perform stretching and folding. Repeatedly fold the dough in half, then press down with your palm to stretch it slightly. Rotate the dough 90 degrees and repeat folding and stretching for 5-10 minutes.
  • Roll Out the Dough: Divide the dough into halves. On a lightly floured surface, roll out each half into a thick sheet, about 1/4-inch thick.
  • Cut the Noodles: Fold the dough sheet loosely and cut it into thick noodles using a sharp knife or a pasta machine attachment.
  • Dust with Flour: Lightly dust the cut noodles with flour to prevent sticking.
  • Bring Water to a Boil: Fill a large pot with water and bring it to a boil.
  • Cook the Noodles: Add the udon noodles to the boiling water. Cook for 8-12 minutes, or until al dente. The noodles will float to the surface when cooked.
  • Drain the Noodles: Using a colander, drain the cooked noodles.
  • Prepare the Dashi Broth: Follow your preferred method for making dashi broth using kombu, katsuobushi (and optionally, shiitake mushrooms) and water. You can also use store-bought dashi broth.
  • Season the Broth (Optional): You can add a touch of mirin, soy sauce, or both to adjust the flavor of the broth to your preference.
  • Heat the Broth: Reheat the dashi broth in a pot until simmering.
  • Assemble the Bowls: Divide the cooked udon noodles among serving bowls. Ladle the hot dashi broth over the noodles. Add optional toppings like sliced scallions and tempura flakes.
  • Udon noodles can also be rinsed under cold running water after cooking to stop the cooking process and achieve a chilled texture for salads or stir-fries.
  • The resting and stretching steps are optional but can improve the chewiness of the noodles.
The beauty of udon lies in its simplicity and versatility. For a comforting hot meal, kake udon reigns supreme. The star of the show is the steaming bowl of dashi broth, its delicate flavor gently embracing the thick and chewy udon noodles. Simple toppings like scallions and tempura flakes add pops of color and texture without overwhelming the palate. This presentation allows the quality of the ingredients and the subtle flavors of the dashi to take center stage. Udon can also be dressed up for a more elaborate presentation. Whether served in a vibrant curry or nestled in a hotpot alongside an array of ingredients, udon's ability to absorb and complement various flavors shines through.

Udon is a delightful option throughout the year. During colder months, a steaming bowl of hot udon offers a comforting and soul-warming experience. The hot broth and chewy noodles provide a satisfying warmth from the inside out, making it a perfect meal for a chilly day. In the warmer months, udon can be enjoyed chilled in refreshing salads or stir-fries. The springy texture of the noodles complements lighter ingredients, offering a delightful and balanced meal that won't leave you feeling weighed down.
